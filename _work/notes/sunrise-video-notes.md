# Norah Jones - Sunrise (Song and Video Notes)

Status: Working notes
Confidence: Mixed
Purpose: Preserve observations, questions, and competing interpretations before writing a polished analysis.

---

# Overall Impression

The song and the video appear to operate somewhat independently.

The song is gentle, emotionally understated, and deliberately nonspecific.

The video introduces surreal symbolism and recurring visual motifs that invite interpretation without clearly resolving into a literal narrative.

The result is that the video feels considerably more enigmatic than the lyrics alone.

---

# Song

## General Tone

The lyrics suggest:

- intimacy
- domesticity
- affection
- quiet endurance
- suspended time

There is very little explicit narrative.

No names.

No genders.

No obvious conflict.

Most emotional meaning comes from implication.

---

## Verse 1

> Sunrise, sunrise looks like morning in your eyes
> But the clock's held 9:15 for hours

Possible interpretations:

- Lovers remain in bed.
- Time feels suspended.
- Subjective rather than literal time.
- Deliberate withdrawal from ordinary life.

---

> Sunrise couldn't tempt us if it tried
> 'Cause the afternoon's already come and gone

Likely suggests:

- ignoring the outside world
- spending an entire day together
- loss of awareness of ordinary time

This interpretation is supported by contemporary reviews describing the song as lovers remaining together throughout the day.

---

## Verse 2

> Surprise, surprise, couldn't find it in your eyes
> But I'm sure it's written all over my face

Observations:

The emotional asymmetry is striking.

The narrator:

- cannot read the other person's emotions
- believes her own emotions are obvious

Possible interpretations:

- vulnerability
- uncertainty
- emotional openness

Little evidence for:

- deception
- lying
- betrayal

---

> We made it through another day

Interesting wording.

Not:

"I'm happy."

Instead:

"We made it."

Possible themes:

- endurance
- gratitude
- ordinary survival
- appreciation of shared life

---

## Bridge

> Now the night will throw its cover down
> On me again

Night appears almost as an active force.

---

> And if I'm right

This qualifier is important.

The narrator is uncertain.

She is expressing either:

- hope
- prediction
- intuition

rather than certainty.

---

> It's the only way
> To bring me back
> To you

Questions:

What does "back" mean?

Possibilities:

- physical reunion
- emotional reconnection
- psychological intimacy
- return from ordinary daytime life

The line remains one of the song's major ambiguities.

# Video

## General Style

Director:

James Frost

The video should be treated as an artistic interpretation rather than a literal illustration of the lyrics.

The imagery is surreal and associative.

---

# The Tree

Most visually striking symbol.

Observations:

- unmistakably vulva-like opening
- fabric folds resemble female anatomy
- people appear within the opening
- camera sometimes looks outward from inside the opening

The last point is particularly significant.

The tree is not merely observed.

The audience repeatedly occupies its perspective.

# Current Conclusion

The song itself remains relatively straightforward:

a quiet, intimate song about shared time and emotional vulnerability.

The video introduces symbolic imagery—especially the tree—that deliberately expands rather than explains the lyrics.

The strongest evidence currently supports:

- suspended time
- intimacy
- sexuality
- emotional uncertainty

The evidence does not currently support confident claims about:

- hidden relationships
- autobiographical meaning
- deception
- specific sexual identities

Further analysis should distinguish carefully between:

- direct observation
- reasonable interpretation
- unsupported speculation.

# References

Here is a source list, ordered from primary material to secondary interpretation. I have not found an interview in which Norah Jones or director James Frost explicitly explains the tree, the woman, or the video’s romantic relationships.

### Primary and production sources

1. [Official “Sunrise” music video — Norah Jones/Blue Note](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=fd02pGJx0s0)
   The primary visual text. Useful for checking exactly when the woman, man, tree opening, fishing poles, clocks, and other images appear.

2. [“Sunrise” official release credits](https://www.dailymotion.com/video/x6vs9m4)
   The label-supplied metadata names James Frost as director, Katherine Kennedy as film producer, and Lee Alexander in the song credits. It also lists the musicians, including backing vocalist Daru Oda.

3. [IMDb full cast and crew](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt9843230/fullcredits/)
   Identifies **Mia Adams as “Singing Tree.”** This is particularly relevant: it indicates that the woman appearing in or as the tree was a credited performer playing a designed video role. It does not identify her as Norah’s lover or as the recording’s backing singer.

4. [IMDb main video record](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt9843230/)
   Confirms that the video was directed by James Frost and features Norah Jones and Mia Adams.

5. [Norah Jones’s official website](https://www.norahjones.com/)
   The appropriate place to check official news and archived artist material. Its currently searchable pages do not appear to contain an explanation of “Sunrise.”

6. [Norah Jones’s Blue Note artist page](https://www.bluenote.com/artist/norah-jones/)
   Official record-label biography and discography context for *Feels Like Home*. It does not presently offer a scene-by-scene interpretation.

### Contemporary interpretations

7. [“Sweet and Sour” — *The New Yorker*, February 23, 2004](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2004/03/01/sweet-and-sour-3)
   A contemporary review calling “Sunrise” sexy and interpreting the clock fixed at 9:15 as bedroom imagery. This supports an erotic interpretation of the song, but it is critic Sasha Frere-Jones’s reading—not Jones’s explanation.

8. [“Slow Burn” — *The New Yorker*, March 29, 2004](https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2004/03/29/slow-burn-2)
   Reads the song as being about lovers remaining together through the morning and afternoon, implicitly associating it with daytime sex. Again, this is criticism rather than an official account.

9. [*Feels Like Home* review — AllMusic](https://www.allmusic.com/album/feels-like-home-mw0000332382)
   Describes “Sunrise” as an “intimate” single. It is useful as a more restrained critical reading, although it does not analyze the tree or video narrative.

### Reference sources

10. [“Sunrise” song reference page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunrise_%28Norah_Jones_song%29)
    Conveniently compiles the songwriting and recording personnel: Norah Jones and Lee Alexander as writers; Adam Levy and Daru Oda as backing vocalists. Its underlying album-booklet citations are more authoritative than the page itself.

11. [*Feels Like Home* album reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feels_like_Home_%28Norah_Jones_album%29)
    Provides release and album context. Use it mainly as a path to the cited contemporary reviews and published credits.

The most important factual distinction in these sources is that **Mia Adams is credited as “Singing Tree,” whereas Daru Oda is a backing vocalist on the recording**. Therefore, describing the woman in the tree as “the backup singer” is not supported by the available credits.

The erotic, vulval, or womb-like interpretation of the tree is visually reasonable, but I would label it as interpretation rather than documented authorial intent. No source above records Jones or Frost saying, for example, “the tree represents a vulva,” or confirming that the video depicts Norah’s character being in love with the woman.
