Yes, and this insight shifts a company's focus from functional engineering to emotional and psychological branding. Theodore Levitt’s core philosophy—most famously encapsulated in his quote, "People don't want to buy a quarter-inch drill. They want a quarter-inch hole!"—forces businesses to realize they are selling solutions and identities, not just physical goods. [1, 2, 3, 4]
## Why Differentiation Proliferates
When a firm stops viewing its product as a commodity and starts viewing it as a vehicle for consumer value, the possibilities for differentiation become virtually limitless.

* Expanded Product Definition: The "product" expands to include the customer service, the delivery speed, the packaging, and the brand reputation.
* Targeting Emotional Needs: Instead of competing purely on price or technical specs, companies can differentiate based on status, peace of mind, community, or self-expression.
* Value Creation: Firms begin solving secondary and tertiary problems for the customer, moving up the value chain from basic utilities to complex experiences. [5]

## The "Total Product Concept" Breakdown
Levitt operationalized this idea through his Total Product Concept, which breaks a product down into four concentric layers. This framework shows exactly where and how proliferation happens: [6, 7]

* The Core Product: The generic, baseline commodity (e.g., a hotel room). [8]
* The Expected Product: The bare minimum customer expectations (e.g., a clean bed, towels, and working Wi-Fi). Competitors easily match this. [9]
* The Augmented Product: Features that go beyond expectations to surprise the customer (e.g., free room service upgrades, personalized streaming profiles). This is where differentiation begins to proliferate. [10]
* The Potential Product: Everything that is feasibly possible to attract and hold customers in the future (e.g., virtual reality room customization, predictive AI concierges). [11]

Ultimately, commoditization lives in the "core" and "expected" layers. Proliferation, innovation, and long-term brand loyalty live entirely in the "augmented" and "potential" spaces, where meaning is constructed.
