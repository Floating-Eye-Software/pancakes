# Purpose

This document provides governance guidance for institutions that steward shared resources.

It explains how commons institutions remain:

* legitimate;
* accountable;
* adaptive;
* participant-governed;
* resilient over time.

This document is **not** a general theory of institutional design.

The Institutional Engineering Methodology determines whether commons governance is appropriate for a particular coordination problem.

This document explains how a commons institution should govern itself once that institutional form has been selected.

---

# Core Thesis

Some coordination problems are best solved through participant-governed commons.

Commons governance succeeds when:

* authority remains accountable;
* stewardship remains transparent;
* participants retain meaningful voice;
* institutions continually learn;
* governance remains proportional to the risks involved.

The objective is not governance for its own sake.

The objective is the long-term stewardship of shared resources in service of human flourishing.

---

# Why Commons Exist

Some resources cannot be managed well by isolated individuals.

Others are poorly served by centralized ownership alone.

Commons emerge where communities share ongoing responsibility for resources that require continual cooperation.

Examples include:

* community gardens;
* cooperative workshops;
* shared housing;
* neighborhood organizations;
* research communities;
* knowledge repositories;
* service exchanges;
* shared digital infrastructure;
* environmental stewardship.

Commons are institutions.

They exist to solve coordination problems involving shared resources and shared responsibility.

---

# When Commons Governance Is Appropriate

Not every institution should be governed as a commons.

Examples where commons governance is often appropriate include:

* cooperatives;
* neighborhood associations;
* makerspaces;
* community food systems;
* service exchanges;
* research collaborations;
* community archives;
* shared infrastructure.

Examples where commons governance may not be appropriate include:

* personal journals;
* private household notes;
* individual creative work;
* single-user applications;
* purely private tools.

Institutional Engineering determines whether commons governance is appropriate.

Commons governance is derived.

It is never assumed.

---

# The Commons

A commons consists of:

* a shared resource;
* a community of participants;
* shared responsibilities;
* shared stewardship;
* governance processes;
* institutional memory.

The resource may be:

* physical;
* digital;
* symbolic;
* informational;
* financial;
* ecological;
* social.

The defining characteristic is not the resource itself.

It is the continuing responsibility to steward it together.

---

# Governance Objectives

Healthy commons pursue several complementary objectives.

They seek to:

* preserve trust;
* protect shared resources;
* support cooperation;
* resolve conflict fairly;
* encourage participation;
* protect vulnerable participants;
* preserve institutional memory;
* adapt through learning;
* sustain the commons over time.

Governance is therefore not merely about making decisions.

It is about maintaining the conditions that allow cooperation to continue.

---

# Stewardship

Commons exist to support stewardship.

Stewardship means accepting responsibility for resources that affect others.

Stewards:

* care for shared resources;
* fulfill commitments;
* protect future participants;
* preserve institutional knowledge;
* improve the commons through experience.

Stewardship is demonstrated through action.

It is not established by title alone.

---

# Authority

Authority does not arise automatically because someone occupies a governance role.

Authority emerges through:

* demonstrated capability;
* fulfilled commitments;
* responsible stewardship;
* community trust.

Commons governance preserves legitimate authority.

It does not create legitimacy by itself.

Authority remains accountable to the community that recognizes it.

---

# Participation

Commons governance depends upon meaningful participation.

Participants should understand:

* how the commons operates;
* what responsibilities exist;
* how decisions are made;
* how disagreements are resolved;
* how governance changes.

Participation does not require every decision to involve every participant.

Different decisions may appropriately use:

* consent;
* delegation;
* elected stewardship;
* expert review;
* facilitated discussion;
* emergency authority.

The process should remain understandable and legitimate.

---

# Boundaries

Healthy commons define clear boundaries.

Participants should understand:

* who belongs to the commons;
* what resources are governed;
* what authority exists;
* what information remains private;
* what information is shared;
* what rights exist;
* what obligations accompany participation.

Clear boundaries reduce conflict and strengthen accountability.

---

# Rules Should Fit Local Conditions

No governance system is universally appropriate.

Rules should reflect:

* local culture;
* available resources;
* participant capacity;
* environmental conditions;
* legal obligations;
* technical capability;
* community priorities.

Communities should avoid copying governance structures simply because they succeeded elsewhere.

Local conditions matter.

---

# Collective Rule Making

People affected by operational rules should have meaningful opportunities to influence those rules.

Communities should define:

* who may propose changes;
* how proposals are reviewed;
* how disagreements are addressed;
* how emergency decisions occur;
* how rules are documented;
* how decisions become effective.

Governance should remain understandable.

Invisible governance is rarely legitimate governance.

---

# Monitoring

Commons require monitoring.

Monitoring protects shared resources.

It should not become surveillance.

Monitoring should therefore be:

* proportionate;
* privacy-preserving;
* accountable;
* transparent in purpose;
* limited to legitimate governance needs.

Evidence should support stewardship rather than dominate participants.

---

# Repair Before Punishment

Most governance failures are not malicious.

Many arise from:

* misunderstanding;
* inexperience;
* overload;
* conflicting expectations;
* changing circumstances.

Healthy commons therefore emphasize:

* clarification;
* mediation;
* education;
* restoration;
* negotiated repair.

Sanctions remain available when necessary.

Repair should normally come first.

---

# Graduated Responses

Not every governance problem deserves the same response.

Communities should distinguish between:

* mistakes;
* misunderstandings;
* negligence;
* repeated free-riding;
* abuse;
* coercion;
* fraud;
* deliberate harm.

Responses should remain proportional.

Escalation should remain reviewable.

Serious harms may require involvement beyond the commons itself.

---

# Conflict Resolution

Disagreement is inevitable.

Healthy commons provide accessible ways to resolve disputes.

Participants should know:

* how to raise concerns;
* who reviews disputes;
* expected timelines;
* confidentiality expectations;
* appeal procedures;
* escalation paths.

Conflict resolution should be timely, understandable, and proportionate.

---

# Rights to Organize

Communities should retain the ability to organize themselves.

Participants should be able to:

* establish institutions;
* define governance appropriate to local conditions;
* revise governance through legitimate processes;
* preserve institutional memory;
* cooperate with other institutions.

External relationships should support rather than unnecessarily constrain local self-governance.

---

# Nested Governance

Responsibility naturally exists at multiple scales.

```text
Individual
        ↓
Household
        ↓
Institution
        ↓
Community
        ↓
Federation
        ↓
External authorities where appropriate
```

Responsibility should normally remain at the smallest competent level.

Broader governance becomes appropriate only when broader coordination genuinely improves outcomes.

Nested governance reduces unnecessary centralization while preserving effective cooperation.

---

# Continuous Learning

Healthy governance continually improves.

Communities should periodically review:

* participant experience;
* stewardship capacity;
* dispute patterns;
* resource health;
* governance workload;
* rule effectiveness;
* changing external conditions.

Learning should produce:

* improvement;
* adaptation;
* institutional memory.

Governance is a living process.

Not a finished design.

---

# Commons Maturity

Commons governance develops gradually.

## Level 1 — Informal

Governance is largely ad hoc.

Responsibilities remain unclear.

---

## Level 2 — Managed

Basic governance processes exist.

Roles and responsibilities are becoming defined.

---

## Level 3 — Operational

Governance processes operate consistently.

Participants understand how the commons functions.

---

## Level 4 — Systematic

Governance is regularly reviewed using evidence.

Improvement becomes routine.

---

## Level 5 — Learning

The commons continually improves through experience.

Knowledge is preserved.

Governance adapts responsibly as conditions change.

---

# Assessing Commons Governance

Healthy commons periodically assess themselves.

Assessment should consider:

* stewardship effectiveness;
* participant trust;
* dispute resolution;
* resource health;
* governance clarity;
* monitoring practices;
* institutional memory;
* improvement activities.

Assessment exists to improve governance.

Not to perform governance.

# Failure Modes

Commons governance can fail in predictable ways.

Examples include:

## Hidden Authority

Informal power replaces accountable governance.

---

## Bureaucratic Accumulation

Governance becomes increasingly difficult to navigate.

---

## Institutional Capture

A small group gradually controls the commons without meaningful accountability.

---

## Performative Participation

Communities appear participatory while meaningful decisions occur elsewhere.

---

## Monitoring Becomes Surveillance

Evidence collection exceeds legitimate stewardship needs.

Participant trust declines.

---

## Rule Accumulation

Rules continue increasing while their purpose becomes unclear.

---

## Loss of Institutional Memory

Communities repeatedly rediscover the same problems because learning is not preserved.

---

## Uniform Governance

Communities copy governance structures without considering local conditions.

Legitimacy gradually declines.

# Success Criteria

A healthy commons enables participants to:

* understand governance;
* influence shared rules;
* steward resources responsibly;
* resolve disagreements fairly;
* preserve institutional memory;
* improve governance through experience;
* cooperate without unnecessary bureaucracy;
* leave without institutional captivity.

The practical test is simple:

> Can the people affected by a shared resource understand it, govern it, repair harm, adapt their rules, preserve their shared knowledge, and cooperate without surrendering authority to an unaccountable center?

Commons governance succeeds when stewardship remains both humane and durable.

# Historical Context

This model draws upon several traditions of institutional thought.

In particular, it is informed by Elinor Ostrom's work on commons governance, quality-management approaches to continual improvement, and broader research into stewardship, institutional legitimacy, and participant governance.

Pancakes extends these ideas into local-first software, digital commons, federated institutions, and modern community infrastructure.

The objective is not to reproduce any historical framework unchanged.

It is to provide practical guidance for designing and sustaining humane commons in the twenty-first century.
