# Escarpment Blues — Sarah Harmer

[Back to the playlist](pancakes-playlist.md) | [Liner notes](pancakes-playlist-liner-notes.md#12-sarah-harmer---escarpment-blues)

## Why this song is in the Pancakes playlist

Many songs in the Pancakes playlist are explicitly political. They speak directly about labour, solidarity, power, poverty, dignity, or social change.

*Escarpment Blues* is different.

It is not mainly about institutions or policy. It is about **place**.

The song asks the listener to consider the value of a landscape before asking what can be extracted from it. It treats the Niagara Escarpment as something with its own integrity, history, and right to continue as a living system.

That perspective is central to Pancakes.

---

## The Escarpment As Relationship

The song was written in the context of public opposition to quarry expansion along the Niagara Escarpment, specifically the proposed Nelson Aggregates expansion on Mount Nemo in north Burlington.

That detail matters. This was not an abstract environmental slogan. It came out of a real place, a real neighbourhood, and a real legal and civic fight over aquifers, habitat, and the future of the escarpment.

Harmer also helped co-found Protecting Escarpment Rural Land, or PERL, to support the opposition effort.

Rather than arguing through technical language, it asks a simpler question:

How should people relate to the places they inhabit?

This is a recurring theme throughout Pancakes.

People do not merely occupy landscapes.

They participate in them.

Communities inherit watersheds, forests, farmland, rivers, wetlands, neighbourhoods, and public spaces. Flourishing depends partly on how those relationships are maintained over time.

---

## The Value Of Ordinary Places

One of the recurring ideas in Pancakes is that ordinary things deserve attention.

Ordinary work.

Ordinary care.

Ordinary communities.

Ordinary places.

The Niagara Escarpment is not presented as an exotic wilderness. It is a familiar landscape that many people pass every day without noticing.

The song invites the listener to look again.

That habit of attention is exactly what Pancakes hopes to cultivate.

---

## Water Rather Than Stone

The most striking image in the song is not really the rock itself.

It is water.

The song emphasizes wells, creeks, aquifers, rainfall, soil, and the conditions that make life possible. The escarpment matters not simply because it is beautiful.

It matters because it participates in a much larger ecological system.

Aquifers, streams, wetlands, forests, wildlife, farms, and communities are all connected.

This reflects the Pancakes place model.

Places are not isolated points on a map.

They are networks of relationships.

---

## On The Treasure Image

The song introduces an image of hidden value deep in the rock, and the key word is **pearl**.

That image has prompted discussion during development of Pancakes.

There is also a likely bit of wordplay here: Harmer was involved with PERL, and PERL and pearl are homophones. Read that way, the metaphor is doing double duty. It names the organizing context around the song while also giving the escarpment an image of hidden worth.

Taken literally, the pearl metaphor is a little odd. Pearls come from living molluscs, not from stone. So the image is not a scientific claim.

But it is still a serious metaphor.

It suggests hidden worth beneath the surface of the escarpment. Read too literally, that can sound extractive, as if the land were hiding a jewel that ought to be dug out and claimed.

The better interpretation is almost the opposite.

The hidden treasure is not another object to remove.

It is the water and the living system that depends on it.

The aquifers beneath the escarpment support ecosystems and communities over enormous spans of time. Their value lies in continued participation within the landscape, not extraction from it.

That reading is more consistent with the broader philosophy of stewardship that runs throughout the project.

The pearl image still matters because it gives the song emotional force. The escarpment is not just useful. It is precious. Pancakes can accept that force while still preferring a stewardship reading over a mining reading.

---

## Stewardship Instead Of Extraction

Throughout the Pancakes ecosystem there is a recurring contrast between two ways of seeing the world.

The first asks:

How much can we take from this place?

The second asks:

How can we care for this place?

Neither question rejects human industry.

Communities need homes, roads, food, energy, and building materials.

The challenge is deciding where extraction is appropriate, where restoration is necessary, and where restraint is the wiser choice.

The song does not offer technical answers.

It instead asks listeners to reconsider the assumptions behind the question, especially the assumption that visible land is only valuable when it can be turned into stone, product, or infrastructure.

That is why the song lands so strongly in Pancakes. It is not anti-development in a simplistic way. It is skeptical of the habit of treating a place as a reserve of parts.

The later legal outcome reinforces that reading. The Burlington-area struggle continued for years, and the province eventually rejected the Nelson expansion in 2012, in part to protect the endangered Jefferson salamander. The song belongs to that kind of place-based civic memory: not a general complaint about quarries, but a specific defense of one landscape.

---

## Place Before Politics

Unlike many protest songs, *Escarpment Blues* rarely argues directly.

Its strategy is quieter.

It encourages affection before policy.

Understanding before legislation.

Relationship before ownership.

Pancakes follows a similar pattern.

The ecosystem is designed to help people become more familiar with their neighbourhoods, watersheds, parks, public spaces, and communities before asking them to improve them.

Stewardship grows more naturally from familiarity than from obligation.

The song also has a local, spoken-from-here quality that matters. It does not present the escarpment as a distant abstraction. It sounds like someone defending the place they know because they live with it.

## Connections To Pancakes

Several later design ideas within Pancakes were influenced by conversations around this song.

These include:

* treating place as a first-class concept rather than simple GPS coordinates
* neighbourhood stewardship instead of location-based rewards
* watershed- and ecosystem-aware symbolic frequencies
* node homelands and local civic knowledge
* open geographic reference services
* symbolic terroir, where place contributes meaning without becoming a competitive advantage

These ideas all begin with the same observation:

Where something happens matters.

Not because some places are worth more than others, but because every place has its own history, ecology, and relationships.

---

## Lone Honk

Although *Escarpment Blues* is part of the Pancakes playlist rather than the Lone Honk soundtrack, it also influenced the design of Lone Honk.

Lone Honk focuses on the ordinary geography of Southern Ontario.

Rather than presenting famous landmarks alone, it encourages players to notice infrastructure, rivers, wetlands, quarries, transit systems, neighbourhoods, and conservation areas.

The Bowmanville quarry, for example, is not simply an industrial location.

It is one visible consequence of the geological history that also produced the Niagara Escarpment.

Understanding those relationships makes both the game world and the real world richer.

---

## Why It Belongs In The Playlist

Most songs in the Pancakes playlist teach something about society.

*Escarpment Blues* teaches something about attention.

It reminds us that flourishing is not built only through better institutions or stronger communities.

It also depends on learning to see the places we already inhabit with greater care.

Before a landscape becomes a resource, a park, a quarry, a watershed, or a political issue, it is simply a place.

Learning to value that place is where stewardship begins.
