# Dosa and the Indus Valley

## Did the People of the Indus Valley Eat Dosa?

One of the most common questions raised by discussions of ancient pancakes is whether the people of the Indus Valley Civilization ate dosa.

The short answer is probably not.

The longer answer is much more interesting.

The Indus Valley Civilization flourished between roughly 3300 BCE and 1300 BCE across what is now Pakistan and northwestern India. It was one of the great early urban civilizations, contemporary with ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia. Its cities included sophisticated drainage systems, standardized weights, long-distance trade networks, and large populations supported by agriculture.

The people of the Indus Valley cultivated wheat, barley, peas, sesame, and other crops. Archaeological evidence suggests that grain processing was widespread and that bread-like foods were important components of the diet.

What we do not have are surviving recipes.

Organic foods rarely survive for thousands of years, and the Indus script remains undeciphered. We know far more about Indus drainage systems than we do about Indus cookbooks.

It is therefore reasonable to assume that the people of the Indus Valley produced some form of grain cakes, flatbreads, or pancake-like foods. Such preparations would have been practical, efficient, and compatible with the ingredients and technologies available.

Dosa, however, appears to be much younger.

The modern dosa is associated primarily with South India and is made from a fermented batter of rice and urad dal. Historical references suggest that dosa-like foods emerged during the first millennium CE, thousands of years after the decline of the Indus Valley Civilization.

The key innovation is fermentation.

A simple grain cake requires only flour, water, and heat.

Dosa requires a more elaborate process. Rice and lentils must be soaked, ground, mixed, and fermented. The resulting batter develops distinctive flavors, textures, and cooking properties.

The dosa therefore represents an important stage in the evolution of pancake technology.

If the earliest pancakes were simple solutions to the problem of turning grain into food, dosa represents a later period of experimentation and refinement. Generations of cooks learned how to harness microbial activity to create something lighter, more complex, and more nutritious.

In this sense, the relationship between Indus grain cakes and dosa may be similar to the relationship between a dugout canoe and a sailing ship.

Both belong to the same broad family.

One is much more sophisticated.

The people of the Indus Valley almost certainly ate foods that modern observers would recognize as relatives of pancakes.

The dosa is one of the descendants of that long tradition, not its ancestor.
