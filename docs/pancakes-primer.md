# Pancakes Primer

## A Gentle Introduction to Commons, Cooperation, and Digital Institutions

**Status:** Introductory reference

**Audience:** Anyone curious about Pancakes, Pitchfork, digital commons, cooperatives, or networked communities.

**Purpose:** Explain the ideas and terminology used throughout the Pancakes and Pitchfork documents without assuming any prior knowledge of economics, governance, political theory, or cryptocurrency.

---

# Why This Document Exists

The Pancakes and Pitchfork documents talk about:

* commons;
* cooperatives;
* polycentric governance;
* network states;
* DAOs;
* protocols;
* federations;
* digital institutions;
* stewardship;
* service exchange; and
* constitutional infrastructure.

Many of these terms come from economics, political science, and technology communities. They can sound intimidating.

They are simply tools for answering a few human questions:

* How do people work together?
* How do they share resources?
* How do they make decisions?
* How do they resolve conflicts?
* How do they prevent abuse?
* How do they care for each other?
* How do they build institutions that survive?

Pancakes is interested in these questions because it aims to support ordinary life, cooperation, care, and shared infrastructure without turning people into products or reducing life to productivity metrics.

---

# A Few Big Ideas

Before defining specific terms, it helps to understand a few themes that appear throughout the project.

## Human life has value beyond markets

Many important activities are difficult to price:

* caring for children;
* supporting friends;
* maintaining a household;
* learning;
* volunteering;
* resting;
* recovering from illness;
* participating in community life.

Pancakes tries to make these forms of participation visible without turning every activity into wage labor or financial speculation.

---

## Technology cannot replace governance

Software can:

* keep records;
* automate simple tasks;
* enforce certain rules;
* provide evidence.

Software cannot automatically answer questions like:

* Was this fair?
* Was someone harmed?
* Should there be an exception?
* Do the rules need to change?

Human communities still need:

* discussion;
* judgment;
* empathy;
* conflict resolution;
* accountability.

---

## Institutions matter

An institution is simply an organized way that people do things together.

Examples:

* families;
* schools;
* churches;
* libraries;
* cooperatives;
* cities;
* governments;
* clubs;
* companies.

Pancakes is interested in building digital tools that support healthy institutions rather than replacing them.

---

# What Is Governance?

Governance means:

> How a group makes decisions and exercises authority.

Governance includes questions like:

* Who can participate?
* Who makes decisions?
* How are rules changed?
* How are disagreements resolved?
* Who is responsible when things go wrong?

Governance is broader than voting.

A family has governance.

A school has governance.

A company has governance.

A friend group has governance.

Any time people coordinate and make decisions together, governance exists.

---

# What Is a Commons?

A commons is:

> A shared resource together with the people and rules that care for it.

Examples:

* a community garden;
* a fishery;
* an irrigation system;
* an open-source software project;
* a shared knowledge base.

A commons is not:

```text
everything belongs to nobody
```

A commons is:

```text
shared resource
+ community
+ rules
+ stewardship
```

Commons require:

* boundaries;
* responsibilities;
* maintenance;
* conflict resolution;
* trust.

A commons can fail if:

* nobody takes responsibility;
* a small group captures control;
* people consume more than they maintain;
* conflicts cannot be resolved.

---

# What Is Stewardship?

Stewardship means:

> Taking care of something so that it remains healthy and useful over time.

Examples:

* maintaining a community garden;
* moderating an online forum;
* helping newcomers;
* repairing shared infrastructure;
* documenting processes.

Stewardship focuses on long-term care rather than short-term extraction.

---

# What Is Cooperation?

Cooperation means:

> People working together toward a shared purpose.

Cooperation often produces outcomes that individuals cannot easily achieve alone.

Examples:

* raising children;
* maintaining a park;
* running a school;
* creating open-source software;
* operating a food cooperative.

Cooperation requires:

* communication;
* trust;
* accountability;
* shared expectations.

---

# What Is Co-production?

Co-production means:

> Important services are produced jointly by many participants.

Education is not simply:

```text
teacher
→ education delivered
```

It is:

```text
teachers
+ students
+ families
+ peers
→ educational outcomes
```

Health is not simply:

```text
doctor
→ health delivered
```

It is:

```text
health professionals
+ patients
+ families
+ communities
→ health outcomes
```

Many aspects of ordinary life work this way.

Household care, learning, mutual aid, and wellness are often co-produced.

This matters because a ledger can record some contributions but cannot fully measure trust, care, emotional support, or human relationships.

---

# What Is a Cooperative?

A cooperative is:

> An organization owned and governed by the people who use it or work in it.

Examples:

* a worker-owned business;
* a housing cooperative;
* a credit union;
* a grocery cooperative.

The basic idea is:

```text
people affected by the organization
→ share ownership and governance
```

Cooperatives attempt to distribute power more broadly than conventional firms.

They can still fail.

Problems can include:

* low participation;
* governance complexity;
* conflict among members;
* slow decision making.

Nevertheless, cooperatives are one important model for democratic institutions.

---

# What Is a Corporation?

A corporation is:

> A legal entity that can own property, enter contracts, hire employees, and accept legal responsibility.

Corporations are tools.

They are not automatically good or bad.

A corporation can be:

* publicly traded;
* privately owned;
* worker owned;
* nonprofit;
* mission driven;
* exploitative.

Legal structures solve certain problems:

* ownership;
* liability;
* employment;
* contracts;
* taxation.

Pancakes does not assume that every institution should be a corporation, but corporations may sometimes be useful.

---

# What Is a Nonprofit?

A nonprofit is:

> An organization that exists for a mission rather than distributing profits to owners.

Examples:

* charities;
* museums;
* educational organizations;
* community service organizations.

Nonprofits can still:

* hire employees;
* own property;
* operate businesses;
* accumulate reserves.

A nonprofit is a legal structure, not a guarantee of good governance.

---

# What Is a DAO?

DAO stands for:

> Decentralized Autonomous Organization.

A DAO is an organization that uses software and internet-based rules to coordinate decisions and resources.

Very roughly:

```text
people
+ software
+ shared rules
+ shared resources
= DAO
```

Many DAOs use:

* blockchains;
* online voting;
* shared treasuries;
* tokens.

People like DAOs because they can:

* coordinate globally;
* reduce dependence on one company;
* make treasury activity visible;
* support experimentation.

DAOs also have risks:

* wealthy participants may dominate decisions;
* voting can oversimplify difficult questions;
* participation may be low;
* legal responsibility may be unclear;
* software bugs may be costly.

Pancakes views DAOs as one possible institutional tool among many.

A DAO is not automatically the best form of governance.

---

# What Is a Blockchain?

A blockchain is:

> A ledger that many computers maintain and verify together.

A blockchain attempts to answer:

* Who owns what?
* What happened?
* Has the record been altered?

People like blockchains because they can provide:

* tamper resistance;
* independent verification;
* auditable history;
* coordination without a central operator.

Blockchains do not automatically provide:

* justice;
* legitimacy;
* healthy governance;
* good community relationships.

A perfectly accurate ledger can still record an unfair decision.

---

# What Is a Smart Contract?

A smart contract is:

> Software that automatically performs certain actions when specified conditions are met.

Examples:

```text
payment received
→ release digital item

vote passes
→ execute approved action
```

Smart contracts are useful when rules are:

* clear;
* bounded;
* well defined.

They are less useful when situations require:

* exceptions;
* context;
* empathy;
* negotiation.

Not every human agreement should become a smart contract.

---

# What Does "Code Is Law" Mean?

"Code is law" is the idea that:

```text
software rules
=
governance
```

Pancakes rejects this idea.

Software can provide:

* records;
* evidence;
* automation;
* constraints.

Human governance still requires:

* consent;
* participation;
* appeals;
* contextual judgment;
* repair.

Technology can support governance.

It does not replace governance.

---

# What Is Polycentric Governance?

Polycentric means:

> Many centers of decision making rather than one center controlling everything.

Examples:

```text
household
↓
neighborhood
↓
municipality
↓
province
↓
country
```

Each level governs certain matters.

No level governs everything.

Polycentric systems can:

* experiment locally;
* adapt to different conditions;
* distribute authority;
* avoid excessive centralization.

They can also experience:

* coordination difficulties;
* duplication;
* disagreements;
* participation fatigue.

Pancakes strongly favors polycentric governance.

---

# What Is a Federation?

A federation is:

> Independent groups that cooperate while preserving local authority.

Examples:

* provinces within a country;
* universities within an educational system;
* open-source projects sharing standards;
* cooperating community organizations.

A federation tries to balance:

```text
independence
+
cooperation
```

Federations work best when they define:

* responsibilities;
* decision-making authority;
* dispute processes;
* exit procedures.

---

# What Is a Protocol?

A protocol is:

> A shared set of rules that allows different participants to coordinate.

Examples:

* email;
* the web;
* accounting standards;
* communication rules.

Protocols answer questions like:

* How do participants identify themselves?
* What information is exchanged?
* How are records represented?
* How do systems interoperate?

Protocols make cooperation possible between independent participants.

---

# What Is a Node?

A node is:

> A local center of authority, operation, and responsibility.

A node may be:

* an individual;
* a household;
* a cooperative;
* an institution;
* a community.

Nodes can cooperate while remaining distinct.

Pancakes prefers:

```text
many cooperating nodes
```

over:

```text
one platform controlling everyone
```

---

# What Is a Network State?

The network-state idea asks:

> Can an internet community become a durable institution with real-world resources and places?

The basic sequence is:

```text
online community
→ institution
→ physical capacity
→ broader recognition
```

Pancakes finds this formation process useful.

Pancakes does not assume:

* diplomatic sovereignty;
* replacing existing states;
* founder authority as legitimacy.

The interesting question is:

> How can digital communities become real, durable, and beneficial institutions?

---

# What Is Exit?

Exit means:

> The ability to leave an institution.

Examples:

* changing platforms;
* leaving an organization;
* migrating to another node;
* exporting your data.

Exit protects people from captivity.

However, not everyone can leave easily.

Money, health, family responsibilities, disability, and technical skill affect the ability to exit.

---

# What Is Voice?

Voice means:

> The ability to participate in improving an institution.

Examples:

* proposing changes;
* voting;
* mediation;
* appeals;
* discussion;
* feedback.

Healthy institutions usually need both:

```text
voice
+
exit
```

Too little voice can create captivity.

Too much reliance on exit can create fragmentation.

---

# What Is Legitimacy?

Legitimacy means:

> People regard authority as appropriate and acceptable.

Legitimacy cannot be created simply by:

* software;
* voting;
* transactions;
* blockchains;
* metrics.

Legitimacy often depends on:

* participation;
* fairness;
* accountability;
* trust;
* transparency;
* responsiveness.

Legitimacy is ultimately a social achievement.

---

# What Is Pancakes Trying to Build?

Pancakes is attempting to build:

> Humane life infrastructure.

The project combines:

* local-first computing;
* cooperative infrastructure;
* shared protocols;
* symbolic accounting;
* privacy protection;
* community governance;
* mutual aid;
* digital stewardship.

The project does not assume:

* one platform should own everyone's data;
* every activity should become a market;
* every relationship should become a transaction;
* software should replace human judgment;
* growth is more important than care.

Instead, Pancakes asks:

> How can people coordinate, care for one another, and build durable institutions without becoming extractable products?

Everything else in the project exists to help answer that question.
