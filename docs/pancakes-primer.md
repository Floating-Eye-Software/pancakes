# Pancakes Primer

## A Gentle Introduction to Humane Institutions, Cooperation, and Local-First Computing

**Status:** Introductory Reference

**Audience:** Anyone curious about Pancakes, Pitchfork, digital commons, local-first computing, community technology, or institutional design.

---

# Why This Document Exists

Pancakes is a large project.

It combines ideas from:

- software engineering;
- economics;
- political science;
- institutional design;
- community governance;
- privacy engineering;
- local-first computing;
- symbolic accounting.

Many readers encounter unfamiliar terminology.

This document introduces the core ideas without assuming prior knowledge.

It is not intended to be comprehensive.

It is intended to make the rest of the documentation approachable.

---

# What Is Pancakes?

Pancakes is an open ecosystem for engineering, operating, and evolving humane institutions.

It helps people coordinate:

- households;
- communities;
- cooperatives;
- schools;
- clinics;
- mutual aid organizations;
- local economies;
- future civic institutions.

Pancakes is not a social network.

It is not a blockchain.

It is not a marketplace.

It is infrastructure that helps communities coordinate while remaining locally governed.

---

# Three Big Ideas

## Institutions Matter

People rarely flourish alone.

Much of life happens inside institutions:

- families;
- schools;
- workplaces;
- cooperatives;
- clubs;
- governments;
- neighborhoods.

Healthy institutions enable people to cooperate.

Unhealthy institutions make cooperation difficult.

Pancakes exists to support healthier institutions.

---

## Software Supports Institutions

Technology can help institutions by providing:

- memory;
- coordination;
- evidence;
- communication;
- accounting.

Technology cannot replace:

- judgment;
- empathy;
- governance;
- legitimacy;
- responsibility.

People govern.

Software assists.

---

## Communities Before Platforms

Pancakes is organized around independently governed communities rather than one global platform.

Communities cooperate voluntarily.

They do not surrender autonomy to participate.

# Authority

Authority is earned through demonstrated stewardship.

Authority is not simply:

- titles;
- software;
- popularity;
- wealth.

Legitimate authority develops through trust.

# Local-First Computing

People should remain able to:

- own their data;
- export their history;
- continue operating offline;
- choose who they cooperate with.

Local-first computing supports institutional independence.
