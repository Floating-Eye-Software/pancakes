# Institutional Pork Barreling

## Bundling Interpretive Authority into Everyday Digital Institutions

**Pancakes Design Note**

**Status:** Exploratory

---

# 0. Purpose

This document proposes the concept of **institutional pork barreling**.

The term describes a pattern in which institutions gradually expand their authority by attaching unrelated interpretive projects to services that participants joined for entirely different purposes.

The proposal is inspired by the political concept of pork barreling.

In politics, unrelated projects are attached to legislation because legislators who need the primary bill must also accept the attached provisions.

A similar phenomenon appears throughout modern digital institutions.

Participants join for one purpose.

The institution quietly acquires authority for many others.

---

# 1. Motivation

Suppose someone joins an institution in order to:

* search the web,
* communicate with friends,
* navigate a city,
* watch videos,
* share photographs,
* store documents.

Most participants believe they are joining institutions organized around those purposes.

Instead, many digital institutions simultaneously undertake additional projects.

They:

* build behavioral models,
* infer relationships,
* estimate political beliefs,
* infer reproductive status,
* estimate mental health,
* predict purchasing behavior,
* optimize engagement,
* classify participants,
* accumulate long-term institutional memory.

These activities are often not the visible purpose of the institution.

Yet they increasingly become central to its operation.

---

# 2. The Original Institution

Every institution has an apparent purpose.

Examples include:

Search

Messaging

Navigation

Education

Medicine

Banking

Libraries

Postal service

Participants develop expectations based upon those purposes.

Those expectations define legitimate roles.

They also define legitimate interpretive authority.

A physician may legitimately investigate illness.

A librarian ordinarily may not.

A teacher may legitimately evaluate learning.

A grocery store ordinarily may not perform psychiatric assessment.

Institutional legitimacy constrains interpretation.

---

# 3. Expansion Through Attachment

Digital institutions increasingly acquire additional authority not by openly redefining themselves but by attaching secondary projects to primary participation.

Examples include:

Joining a messaging platform becomes participation in long-term behavioral modeling.

Using navigation software becomes participation in commercial prediction.

Searching the web becomes participation in advertising optimization.

Watching videos becomes participation in psychological profiling.

The participant cannot realistically obtain the primary institutional service without simultaneously entering the secondary interpretive project.

This resembles institutional pork barreling.

The additional authority rides along with the original institution.

---

# 4. Consent Is Not Sufficient

These expansions are often justified through consent.

Participants accept lengthy terms of service.

However, institutional legitimacy raises a different question.

Consent may authorize participation.

It does not automatically redefine the legitimate jurisdiction of an institution.

A participant may sign an agreement permitting a grocery store to conduct psychiatric evaluations.

The agreement alone does not transform grocery shopping into psychiatric practice.

Institutions possess purposes independent of contractual language.

Those purposes help define legitimate authority.

---

# 5. Jurisdiction Creep

Institutional pork barreling gradually expands epistemic jurisdiction.

Institutions originally organized around one purpose begin exercising authority over increasingly unrelated aspects of participants' lives.

Examples include:

* reproductive inference,
* psychological profiling,
* political classification,
* relationship prediction,
* economic forecasting,
* health estimation.

Each individual expansion may appear modest.

Collectively they transform the institution into something fundamentally different from the institution participants believed they had joined.

---

# 6. Hidden Participation

Participants generally experience only the visible institution.

For example:

"I am searching."

"I am chatting."

"I am navigating."

Meanwhile a second institution is simultaneously operating.

"I am participating in a long-term interpretive organization."

This second institution often remains invisible.

Its existence becomes apparent only when participants encounter:

* unexpectedly personal advertising,
* highly specific recommendations,
* public reporting,
* regulatory investigations,
* data breaches,
* institutional disclosures.

The participant discovers that they have long occupied a role within an interpretive organization they never consciously joined.

---

# 7. Institutional Memory

These interpretive projects become increasingly significant because they persist.

Institutions rarely perform isolated observations.

Instead they accumulate:

* observations,
* classifications,
* predictions,
* corrections,
* confidence estimates,
* historical models.

Over time this becomes institutional memory.

Future organizational decisions increasingly operate through these accumulated models.

The institution gradually develops an enduring understanding of each participant.

---

# 8. The Problem Is Not Intelligence

The concern is not artificial intelligence.

Nor is it statistical inference.

Nor is it recommendation systems.

Interpretation is unavoidable.

Every institution necessarily develops some understanding of its participants.

The question is one of scope.

How much interpretive authority legitimately belongs to a particular institution?

A fertility clinic should understand fertility.

A psychiatrist should understand mental health.

A search engine was not historically understood as a reproductive institution, a psychiatric institution, or a political institution.

Institutional pork barreling occurs when interpretive authority expands far beyond the institution's visible social purpose.

---

# 9. Governance Implications

Institutional governance should ask not only:

* What data may be collected?
* What models may be built?
* What predictions may be made?

It should also ask:

> Does this interpretive project belong within the legitimate jurisdiction of this institution?

Computational capability alone should not determine legitimacy.

Commercial opportunity alone should not determine legitimacy.

Interpretive authority should remain bounded by institutional purpose.

# 10. Relationship to Other Pancakes Work

This document builds upon several existing governance concepts.

**Epistemic Jurisdiction**

Institutional pork barreling provides one mechanism by which such hidden interpretive organizations arise.

---

# 11. Open Questions

Can institutions voluntarily constrain their own interpretive authority?

Should every interpretive project require independent justification?

Can participants meaningfully reject secondary interpretive projects while remaining members of the primary institution?

Should institutional jurisdiction automatically expire when its original purpose has been fulfilled?

Can AI systems recognize when they are expanding institutional authority beyond legitimate bounds?

How should society evaluate institutions whose primary product has become interpretation rather than the visible service participants originally sought?

---

# 12. Closing Thoughts

Modern institutions increasingly possess extraordinary computational capabilities.

The temptation is to treat every observable pattern as an opportunity for additional prediction, optimization, and organizational knowledge.

Institutional pork barreling suggests a different perspective.

The central question is not whether an institution *can* undertake a particular interpretive project.

Nor whether contractual language can be written broadly enough to permit it.

The more fundamental question is whether that interpretive project belongs to the institution at all.

Humane computing may therefore require more than privacy protections.

It may require institutions to exercise epistemic restraint by refusing interpretive authority that falls outside the legitimate purposes for which society recognizes and participates in those institutions.

A search engine need not become a psychiatrist.

A messaging platform need not become a reproductive institution.

A navigation service need not become a political profiler.

Not because such interpretations are technically impossible.

But because institutions derive legitimacy from remaining faithful to the social purposes that justify their existence in the first place.
