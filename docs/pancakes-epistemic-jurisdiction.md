# Epistemic Jurisdiction

## Institutions, Roles, and Legitimate Interpretive Authority

**Pancakes Design Note**

**Status:** Exploratory

---

# 0. Purpose

This document explores the concept of **epistemic jurisdiction** within the Pancakes ecosystem.

It proposes that legitimate institutions allocate not only authority to act, but also authority to observe, interpret, model, and operationalize knowledge about other participants.

The document is exploratory.

It proposes no normative governance rules.

Instead, it attempts to identify a recurring structure that appears across medicine, education, law, friendship, caregiving, research, and ordinary social life.

---

# 1. Motivation

Many ethical questions in Pancakes cannot be answered solely through existing concepts such as:

* privacy
* consent
* ownership
* confidentiality
* access control
* data sovereignty

Consider the following situations.

A psychiatrist constructs an increasingly accurate model of a patient's inner life.

A physician infers a hidden illness.

A teacher identifies a learning disability.

A parent notices a child's emotional distress.

A caregiver recognizes early cognitive decline.

These activities are not merely tolerated.

They are often expected.

Now consider a different situation.

A casual classmate gradually develops a detailed model of another student's reproductive cycle.

The information may have been derived entirely from public observations.

No private records were accessed.

No deception occurred.

Many people nevertheless experience the second situation as inappropriate.

Why?

This document proposes that the difference is not primarily informational.

It is institutional.

---

# 2. Institutions Allocate Interpretive Authority

Institutions are often understood as allocating:

* authority,
* responsibility,
* accountability,
* obligations,
* permissions.

This document proposes that institutions also allocate:

* legitimate observation,
* legitimate interpretation,
* legitimate inference,
* legitimate prediction,
* legitimate operationalization.

These collectively form an institution's **epistemic jurisdiction**.

Epistemic jurisdiction defines the kinds of interpretive projects that are legitimate for participants occupying particular institutional roles.

---

# 3. Roles and Jurisdiction

Roles are more than collections of responsibilities.

They also define legitimate ways of knowing.

A physician is expected to construct detailed physiological models of patients.

A psychiatrist is expected to construct increasingly sophisticated models of psychological organization.

A teacher is expected to understand a student's learning process.

An employer is expected to understand job performance.

These roles differ not because they possess different intelligence.

They differ because society grants them different interpretive jurisdictions.

---

# 4. The Same Information, Different Jurisdictions

The legitimacy of knowledge often depends on institutional context rather than informational content.

Consider knowledge about a person's reproductive health.

Within reproductive medicine:

such knowledge is expected.

Within a therapeutic relationship:

it may become relevant.

Between close friends:

it may arise naturally through mutual support.

Within an educational institution:

it is ordinarily unrelated to the purposes of the institution.

Within an employment relationship:

it is generally outside legitimate managerial concern.

The information has not changed.

The institutional jurisdiction has.

---

# 5. Observation Is Not Modeling

Observation is an ordinary feature of social life.

People notice:

* conversations,
* clothing,
* schedules,
* emotions,
* habits,
* routines.

Most observations remain transient.

Interpretive projects are different.

An interpretive project is a sustained effort to construct increasingly accurate models of another participant.

Such projects involve:

* accumulating observations,
* organizing patterns,
* refining explanations,
* updating predictions,
* preserving knowledge over time.

Interpretive projects therefore represent a distinct form of participation.

Not every institutional role authorizes them.

---

# 6. Situational Legitimacy

The same interpretive activity may be legitimate in one situation and inappropriate in another.

A psychiatrist is expected to notice subtle recurring psychological patterns.

A fertility specialist is expected to understand reproductive cycles.

A lawyer is expected to understand legal risk.

A teacher is expected to understand educational development.

Each role possesses different epistemic jurisdiction because each institution serves different purposes.

The legitimacy arises from the situation rather than from the cognitive act itself.

---

# 7. Multiple Institutional Membership

People simultaneously occupy many institutional roles.

For example, the same individual may be:

* a physician,
* a parent,
* a spouse,
* a manager,
* a friend,
* a volunteer.

This helps explain familiar expressions such as:

> "As your lawyer..."

or

> "As your friend..."

These phrases are not rhetorical devices.

They identify which institutional role is currently speaking.

Different roles legitimately optimize for different purposes.

Different roles possess different interpretive authority.

The same individual may therefore provide different advice without contradiction.

---

# 8. Interpretive Projects in Pancakes

Pancakes is unusual because it enables long-term reflection.

Users may:

* journal,
* record observations,
* identify patterns,
* review years of experiences,
* receive AI assistance.

This creates a new possibility.

Software may gradually transform ordinary observations into durable interpretive organizations.

These organizations may concern:

* oneself,
* households,
* friendships,
* coworkers,
* communities,
* family members,
* patients,
* students.

The ethical question is not simply whether the resulting models are accurate.

It is whether the interpretive project itself falls within the legitimate jurisdiction established by the surrounding institution.

---

# 9. AI and Epistemic Jurisdiction

Artificial intelligence introduces a new challenge.

AI can often continue interpretive projects beyond what humans would naturally perform.

It may:

* accumulate observations indefinitely,
* detect subtle statistical regularities,
* preserve knowledge without forgetting,
* continually refine models.

Computational capability alone should not determine legitimacy.

Instead, AI should inherit epistemic jurisdiction from the institution and role within which it operates.

For example:

A therapeutic AI may legitimately participate in therapeutic interpretation.

A study-group assistant should not begin constructing reproductive models of classmates simply because such models are computationally possible.

A household assistant may reasonably infer grocery requirements.

It should not automatically infer psychiatric diagnoses of visitors.

Institutional legitimacy should constrain interpretive ambition.

---

# 10. Governance Implications

If epistemic jurisdiction is a meaningful concept, governance extends beyond data access.

Institutions may need to specify:

* what observations are appropriate,
* what long-term models are appropriate,
* what inferences are appropriate,
* what predictions are appropriate,
* what operational decisions may rely upon those models.

This suggests that governance concerns not only information flows but interpretive authority.

# 11. Relationship to Existing Pancakes Work

This document complements several existing areas of the Pancakes ecosystem.

**Institutional Legitimacy**

explains how institutions acquire authority and define legitimate participation.

**Informational Personal Space**

explains why durable modeling of another person may feel invasive even when no privacy boundary has been crossed.

**Hidden Situations**

explains the experience of discovering that one has unknowingly occupied a role within another participant's interpretive organization.

**Privacy Questions**

examines how life-computing systems may inadvertently construct such interpretive organizations.

The present document asks a different question.

Rather than asking why people experience unwanted modeling as invasive, it asks:

> Under what institutional circumstances is one participant legitimately authorized to undertake an interpretive project concerning another participant?

---

# 12. Open Questions

Many questions remain unresolved.

Can epistemic jurisdiction be delegated?

Can it be revoked?

Can it be shared?

Can reciprocal relationships create reciprocal jurisdiction?

Can institutional jurisdiction exceed individual jurisdiction?

Should AI ever possess greater interpretive authority than the humans operating it?

Can symbolic projection preserve useful understanding while reducing unnecessary interpretive expansion?

How should systems recognize that an interpretive project has exceeded the jurisdiction granted by the surrounding institution?

---

# 13. Closing Thoughts

Privacy governs access to information.

Epistemic jurisdiction concerns something different.

It concerns the legitimacy of sustained interpretation.

Modern software increasingly makes it possible to construct rich, persistent models of other people from ordinary observations accumulated over long periods of time.

The ethical question is therefore not merely:

> Who may know?

Nor simply:

> Who may access?

It is increasingly:

> Who is legitimately authorized to undertake particular interpretive projects, within which institutions, under which roles, and for whose benefit?

As life-computing systems become more capable, this question may prove as important as privacy itself.

Rather than limiting knowledge because it is technically possible, humane systems may instead recognize that interpretive authority, like legal authority or medical authority, is situated within legitimate social institutions and bounded by the roles those institutions define.
